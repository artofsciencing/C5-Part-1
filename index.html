💥
COLLISION THEORY
Reactions only happen when particles collide with sufficient energy (≥ activation energy) and the correct orientation.
Rate ∝ Frequency of Successful Collisions
Activation Energy (Ea) — minimum energy needed for a collision to result in a reaction.

Even in fast reactions, only a small fraction of collisions lead to products. We can increase the rate by increasing the number of successful collisions per second.
🌡️
TEMPERATURE
Increasing temperature gives particles more kinetic energy → move faster → more frequent collisions → more exceed activation energy.

🔺 Every +10°C ≈ doubles the reaction rate (approximate rule).
At higher temperatures, the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution shifts right — a much larger fraction of molecules now have energy ≥ Ea. This is why temperature has such a dramatic effect on rate.
🧪
CONCENTRATION & PRESSURE
Higher concentration (solutions) or pressure (gases) → more particles in same volume → more frequent collisions → faster rate.
Solutions: mol/dm³ — doubling concentration roughly doubles collision frequency.

Gases: compressing gas increases pressure, reducing volume → same number of molecules in smaller space → more collisions.
⚗️
SURFACE AREA
Smaller particle size → larger surface area exposed → more collisions at the surface → faster rate.

💡 Powder reacts faster than a lump of the same mass.
Only molecules at the surface of a solid can react. Grinding a solid into powder dramatically increases the available surface area for reactant particles to collide with.
🔑
CATALYSTS
A catalyst lowers the activation energy — provides an alternative reaction pathway. It is not consumed in the reaction.

✅ Same reactants & products, but faster rate.
Enzymes are biological catalysts. Industrial examples:
• Iron → Haber Process (ammonia)
• Platinum → catalytic converters
• Nickel → hydrogenation of vegetable oils

⇌ Reversible Reactions & Equilibrium
Some reactions can go both ways simultaneously
⇌
DYNAMIC EQUILIBRIUM
In a closed system, a reversible reaction reaches equilibrium when the forward rate = reverse rate. The concentrations of reactants and products remain constant — but both reactions are still happening!
A + B ⇌ C + D
⚠️ Equilibrium ≠ equal amounts — the position just doesn't change over time.
📐
LE CHATELIER'S PRINCIPLE
If a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it will shift to counteract the change.
ChangeEquilibrium Shift
🔺 Temperature→ Endothermic direction
🔻 Temperature→ Exothermic direction
🔺 Pressure→ Fewer gas moles side
🔺 Concentration→ Consumes that substance
🏭
THE HABER PROCESS
N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) ΔH = −92 kJ/mol
Temperature: 450°C (compromise — high enough for speed, not so high yield suffers)

Pressure: 200 atm (high pressure → more NH₃)

Catalyst: Iron — speeds up reaction without affecting equilibrium position
♻️ Atom Economy & Percentage Yield
⚖️
ATOM ECONOMY
How efficiently a reaction uses atoms — what percentage end up in the desired product.
Atom Economy = (Mr of desired product ÷ Mr of all products) × 100%
Higher atom economy = greener chemistry ✅
📈
PERCENTAGE YIELD
Compares how much product you actually got vs the theoretical maximum.
% Yield = (Actual yield ÷ Theoretical yield) × 100%
Yield < 100% due to: reversibility, side reactions, incomplete reactions, losses in transfer.
